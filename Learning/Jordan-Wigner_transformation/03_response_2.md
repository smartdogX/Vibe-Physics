## 3. The $Z_2$ Symmetry Of The Kitaev Chain

Now let us do the next topic you asked for.

The most important symmetry of the Kitaev chain is **fermion parity**. This is a $Z_2$ symmetry.

The big picture is:

- ordinary particle-number conservation is broken by superconducting pairing,
- but number mod 2 is still conserved.

So the Kitaev chain does **not** have a full $U(1)$ symmetry generated by particle number, but it **does** have a $Z_2$ symmetry generated by fermion parity.

### 3.1 Start From The Hamiltonian

Write the open-chain Kitaev Hamiltonian as

$$
H
=-t \sum_{j=1}^{L-1}\left(c_j^\dagger c_{j+1}+c_{j+1}^\dagger c_j\right)+ \Delta \sum_{j=1}^{L-1}\left(c_j c_{j+1}+c_{j+1}^\dagger c_j^\dagger\right)- \mu \sum_{j=1}^{L}\left(n_j-\frac12\right).
$$

The hopping term preserves particle number.

The chemical-potential term also preserves particle number.

But the pairing term

$$
c_j c_{j+1} + c_{j+1}^\dagger c_j^\dagger
$$

changes particle number by $\pm 2$, so total particle number is not conserved.

That is why the $U(1)$ symmetry is gone.

### 3.2 Fermion Parity Operator

Define the total fermion number

$$
N = \sum_{j=1}^{L} n_j.
$$

Then define the fermion parity operator

$$
P = (-1)^N = e^{i\pi N}.
$$

Using $n_j^2=n_j$, this can also be written as

$$
P = \prod_{j=1}^{L} (1-2n_j).
$$

This is a $Z_2$ operator because

$$
P^2 = 1.
$$

So its eigenvalues are only

$$
P = +1 \quad \text{or} \quad P=-1,
$$

which correspond to

- even fermion parity,
- odd fermion parity.

That is the meaning of the $Z_2$ symmetry: there are only two parity sectors.

### 3.3 How Parity Acts On Fermion Operators

The key transformation is

$$
P c_j P^{-1} = -c_j,
\qquad
P c_j^\dagger P^{-1} = -c_j^\dagger.
$$

But

$$
P n_j P^{-1} = n_j.
$$

So parity flips the sign of any operator with an odd number of fermion operators, and leaves even-fermion operators unchanged.

This is why every term in the Kitaev Hamiltonian is parity even:

- hopping has two fermion operators,
- pairing has two fermion operators,
- number has two fermion operators through $n_j=c_j^\dagger c_j$.

Therefore

$$
[H,P]=0.
$$

That commutator is the precise statement that fermion parity is a symmetry.

### 3.3.1 How Parity Acts On Majorana Operators And The Edge-State Doublet

This is the missing bridge between the $Z_2$ symmetry and the Majorana picture.

Recall the Majorana operators

$$
\gamma_{A,j} = c_j + c_j^\dagger,
\qquad
\gamma_{B,j} = i(c_j^\dagger - c_j).
$$

Since parity acts as

$$
P c_j P^{-1} = -c_j,
\qquad
P c_j^\dagger P^{-1} = -c_j^\dagger,
$$

it follows immediately that

$$
P \gamma_{A,j} P^{-1} = -\gamma_{A,j},
\qquad
P \gamma_{B,j} P^{-1} = -\gamma_{B,j}.
$$

So each Majorana operator is **odd** under fermion parity.

That is the operator-level statement of how the $Z_2$ symmetry acts on Majoranas: parity flips the sign of every Majorana operator.

Now let us connect that to the edge mode.

In the topological phase of an open chain, the two edge Majoranas approximately define a nonlocal fermion

$$
f = \frac{\gamma_L + i\gamma_R}{2},
\qquad
f^\dagger = \frac{\gamma_L - i\gamma_R}{2},
$$

where you can think of

$$
\gamma_L \approx \gamma_{A,1},
\qquad
\gamma_R \approx \gamma_{B,L}.
$$

Because each Majorana is parity odd, the nonlocal fermion is also parity odd:

$$
P f P^{-1} = -f,
\qquad
P f^\dagger P^{-1} = -f^\dagger.
$$

Suppose $|0\rangle$ is a ground state with definite parity

$$
P|0\rangle = p |0\rangle,
\qquad
p=\pm 1.
$$

Then the occupied edge-mode state is

$$
|1\rangle = f^\dagger |0\rangle.
$$

Acting with parity gives

$$
P|1\rangle
=P f^\dagger P^{-1} \, P|0\rangle
=(-f^\dagger)(p|0\rangle)
=-p\,|1\rangle.
$$

So $|1\rangle$ has the **opposite parity** from $|0\rangle$.

This is the state-level statement:

- the two nearly degenerate edge-mode states differ by the occupation of the nonlocal fermion,
- and that occupation flips the $Z_2$ parity eigenvalue.

There is also an important subtlety here.

Strictly speaking, it is better to talk about:

- Majorana **operators** $\gamma_L,\gamma_R$,
- and parity eigenstates $|0\rangle, |1\rangle$,

than to talk about a single "Majorana state."

Why?

Because a Majorana operator is parity odd:

$$
P \gamma_L P^{-1} = -\gamma_L.
$$

So acting with $\gamma_L$ on a parity eigenstate changes its parity:

$$
P(\gamma_L|0\rangle)
=(P\gamma_L P^{-1})(P|0\rangle)
=(-\gamma_L)(p|0\rangle)
=-p\,(\gamma_L|0\rangle).
$$

That means a Majorana operator maps one parity sector to the other. It does **not** preserve a fixed parity sector.

So the clean statement is:

- the $Z_2$ symmetry flips the sign of Majorana operators,
- and the occupation of the Majorana edge mode toggles the parity of the physical state.

### 3.4 Why Pairing Breaks $U(1)$ But Preserves $Z_2$

This is an important conceptual point.

If a term changes particle number by one, then even parity and odd parity get mixed.

If a term changes particle number by two, parity does not change.

That is exactly what the pairing term does:

- it creates two fermions,
- or annihilates two fermions,
- so $N$ changes,
- but $N \bmod 2$ does not.

So:

- particle number is not conserved,
- fermion parity is conserved.

This is the simplest way to understand the symmetry reduction

$$
U(1) \longrightarrow Z_2.
$$

### 3.5 Relation To The Spin-Chain $Z_2$ Symmetry

Under Jordan-Wigner,

$$
1-2n_j = -\sigma_j^z,
$$

so the fermion parity operator becomes

$$
P = \prod_{j=1}^{L}(1-2n_j)
=\prod_{j=1}^{L}(-\sigma_j^z)
=(-1)^L \prod_{j=1}^{L}\sigma_j^z.
$$

The overall factor $(-1)^L$ is just a constant and does not matter physically, so the symmetry is the same as the global spin-flip operator

$$
U_{\text{spin}} = \prod_{j=1}^{L}\sigma_j^z.
$$

Its action is

$$
U_{\text{spin}}\,\sigma_j^x\,U_{\text{spin}}^{-1} = -\sigma_j^x,
\qquad
U_{\text{spin}}\,\sigma_j^z\,U_{\text{spin}}^{-1} = \sigma_j^z.
$$

So in the Ising language, the $Z_2$ symmetry flips every spin in the $x$ direction.

This is a very important bridge:

- spin language: global Ising spin-flip symmetry,
- fermion language: fermion-parity symmetry.

They are the same symmetry written in two different variables.

### 3.6 Why This Symmetry Matters For Majorana Physics

In the topological phase of an open chain, the two edge Majoranas combine into a nonlocal fermionic mode.

Occupying or emptying that mode changes the fermion parity:

- one state is even,
- the other is odd.

So the near-degenerate ground-state pair lives in two different parity sectors.

Because parity is conserved, a parity-even perturbation cannot directly mix those two states.

That is part of why the Majorana edge mode is robust.

For a finite chain, the two edge Majoranas have a tiny overlap, so the degeneracy is only approximate.

In the long-chain limit, that splitting becomes exponentially small.

## 4. One Important Distinction: Two Different "$Z_2$" Ideas

This point is easy to mix up, so it is worth separating clearly.

There are two different uses of "$Z_2$" around the Kitaev chain:

1. **$Z_2$ symmetry**

This means the conserved fermion parity operator

$$
P = (-1)^N.
$$

2. **$Z_2$ topological classification**

This means that, in the appropriate symmetry class, the bulk phase is either

- trivial,
- or topological.

These two ideas are related, but they are not the same thing.

For your current stage, the symmetry to focus on is the first one:

$$
\text{fermion parity}.
$$

## 5. Synthesis

Here is the compact picture:

- The pairing term destroys particle-number conservation.
- But it changes particle number only by two.
- So fermion parity is still conserved.
- That conserved parity is the $Z_2$ symmetry of the Kitaev chain.
- Under Jordan-Wigner, this becomes the global Ising spin-flip symmetry.
- In the topological phase, the edge Majorana mode connects states of opposite parity.
